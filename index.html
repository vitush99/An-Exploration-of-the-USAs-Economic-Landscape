Background

The economy of the United States is incredibly diverse, with each state contributing uniquely to the nation's overall economic health. From vibrant urban hubs with concentrated wealth to rural regions defined by specific industries, the economic landscape varies dramatically. Explore Household Income

Household income is a critical indicator of economic well-being, reflecting both individual earning potential and the broader socio-economic factors influencing families and individuals. The composition of a household—whether it’s a family with dual incomes, a single-parent household, or an individual living alone—plays a significant role in shaping income levels. By analyzing household income across different categories, we can uncover patterns of economic opportunity, disparities, and resilience, offering a window into the diverse financial realities faced by Americans.

Household median income is very different, depending on the household type and reflects a larger socio-economic dynamic within the United States. In general, a family household with a married couple at its head consistently shows the highest median income levels. Much of this likely comes from a dual-income advantage. By contrast, single-income households, especially those maintained by a single female or male householder with no spouse present, notably lower medians. Such a wide gap in income between family and non-family households indicates the economic struggle of single-parent families due to various systemic factors, including the pay gap between genders and the limited availability of affordable childcare.

Non-family households, including those maintained by a single individual, also have significantly lower median incomes compared to family households. These figures point toward the economic vulnerability of individuals living alone or without familial supporting structures. Furthermore, when viewed across years 2022 and 2023, any observed change in median income across these categories could reflect broader economic trends such as inflation, wage growth, or post-pandemic recovery.

In all, this visualization shows that understanding household composition is crucial for analyzing income inequality. As such, targeted policy interventions are warranted to support single-parent families and reduce wage disparities in order to decrease economic inequities and promote financial resiliency among all household types.

After playing around with the visual, you may realize that there is a relationship between median income and age. This relationship allows us to better understand the life cycle of an individual's career. Income increases through early adulthood and peaks during the 35-54 age range before starting to decline. On the other hand, the 15-24 age bracket has the smallest median income. Paralleling that, the decrease in median income across the age 65-and-over group represents their movement into the retired stage of life when they would be dependent mostly on fixed incomes, like pensions or Social Security, rather than active wages.

The income plateau and subsequent decline beyond age 55 give rise to important considerations of economic security in older age. This finding underlines the importance of ample retirement savings and social support to ensure economic security in later life. It also may be informative to compare the 2022 and 2023 age-income distributions for evidence of how economic conditions (inflation and/or labor market changes) affect different age groups over time.

When you hover over each state, you might notice something interesting: unemployment rates have been climbing since 2023. So, what’s going on here? Well, part of it is that more people are returning to the workforce after taking a break since 2021(post Covid-19), which naturally increases the labor supply. But there’s another big factor at play: the rise of AI. AI has been advancing so quickly in recent years and has excelled in automating repetitive, predictable tasks. Jobs that used to require humans are now being done more efficiently by machines, which means fewer people are needed for the same amount of work. You may have also noticed that states like Nebraska and Utah boast the lowest unemployment rates. These states often have diverse economies and fewer people, which can make it easier to keep unemployment low. For example, states relying heavily on tourism or oil production can face big swings when demand drops or unexpected events happen. Meanwhile, neighboring states might have totally different unemployment rates, which often comes down to differences in local leadership, education systems, or infrastructure investments.

This map is a call to action: smarter economic policies and workforce programs are needed to create equal job opportunities and tackle the disparities we see across the country.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and Gross State Product (GSP)

The economy is a massive and complex system, but two key indicators help us understand its performance: GDP (Gross Domestic Product) at the national level and GSP (Gross State Product) at the state level. Together, they offer a way to measure economic activity, compare growth, and identify disparities both within and across states. This section breaks down these concepts, highlights their differences, and explains their significance in painting a picture of the U.S. economy.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

  • Definition: GDP measures the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country’s borders in a specific time period, usually annually or quarterly.
  • Scope: National level (e.g., the entire United States).
  • Purpose:
    • Provides a snapshot of the overall economic health of a country.
    • Used for comparing the economic performance of different countries.
  • Components:
    • Consumption: Spending by households.
    • Investment: Spending on capital goods like machinery, buildings, etc.
    • Government Expenditures: Government spending on goods and services.
    • Net Exports: Exports minus imports.
  • Example: The GDP of the United States in 2024 is approximately $28.78 trillion.

Gross State Product (GSP)

  • Definition: GSP measures the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a specific state in a given period. It is the state-level equivalent of GDP, measuring economic performance at a sub-national level.
  • Scope: State level (e.g., California, Texas).
  • Purpose:
    • Reflects the economic contribution of individual states to the national economy.
    • Useful for comparing the economic performance of different states within the same country.
  • Components: Same as GDP, but limited to a single state’s economy.
  • Example: Texas’s GSP in 2023 is approximately $2.6 trillion.

Key Differences Between GDP and GSP

Feature GDP GSP
Geographic Focus National (entire country) State (individual states)
Level of Aggregation Macro-level (global comparisons) Micro-level (regional comparisons)
Use Case Compare countries or track overall national economic trends. Assess state-level contributions and disparities.

In essence, GSP is to a state what GDP is to a country. If you are analyzing economic data for individual states within a country (like the United States), the term GSP is appropriate. However, when referring to the aggregated economic performance of the entire country, you use GDP.

  • United Kingdom and France: These countries maintain strong GDP figures due to their established industries, including finance, fashion, and tourism. They represent stability in a rapidly changing global economy.
  • Brazil: Brazil’s bubble may shrink and expand slightly, but its presence is due to its rich natural resources, agriculture, and growing industrial base.
  • Canada: With a smaller population but vast natural resources, Canada holds its ground as a top economy. Its stable financial systems and trade relations with the U.S. help it stay competitive.
  • South Korea: Known for its tech giants like Samsung and LG, South Korea punches above its weight with its innovation-driven economy and strong export markets.

Each bubble tells its own story. While the United States remains a steady and dominant force, the rise of countries like China and India showcases the shift in global economic power towards Asia. The resilience of countries like Japan, Germany, and South Korea highlights the importance of innovation and adaptability.
