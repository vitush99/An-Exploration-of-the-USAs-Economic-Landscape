Explore Household Income

Household income is a critical indicator of economic well-being, reflecting both individual earning potential and the broader socio-economic factors influencing families and individuals. The composition of a household—whether it’s a family with dual incomes, a single-parent household, or an individual living alone—plays a significant role in shaping income levels. By analyzing household income across different categories, we can uncover patterns of economic opportunity, disparities, and resilience, offering a window into the diverse financial realities faced by Americans.

Use the visualization below to compare head to head houshold incomes and compare between the years 2022 and 2023. **Make sure to uncheck plot all categories if you want to compare head to head!

2022 2023

Household median income is very different, depending on the household type and reflects a larger socio-economic dynamic within the United States. In general, a family household with a married couple at its head consistently shows the highest median income levels. Much of this likely comes from a dual-income advantage. By contrast, single-income households-most especially those maintained by a single female or male householder with no spouse-present notably lower medians. Such a wide gap in income between family and non-family households indicates the economic struggle of single-parent families due to various systemic factors, including the pay gap between genders and the limited availability of affordable childcare.

Non-family households, therefore, including those maintained by a single individual, also have significantly lower median incomes compared to family households. These figures point toward the economic vulnerability of individuals living alone or without familial supporting structures. Furthermore, when viewed across years-2022 and 2023-any observed change in median income across these categories could reflect broader economic trends such as inflation, wage growth, or post-pandemic recovery. For example, even the minor increase in incomes for most types may point to an incremental economic increase, although the continuing dispersion among household types indicates persisting structural inequity.

In all, this visualization shows that understanding household composition is crucial for analyzing income inequality. As such, targeted policy interventions are warranted to support single-parent families and reduce wage disparities in order to decrease economic inequities and promote financial resiliency among all household types.

Exploring Incomes by Age Groups

Income levels are closely tied to an individual’s age, reflecting the progression of careers, educational attainment, and retirement. Understanding these trends reveals not only the earning potential across different stages of life but also the economic challenges and opportunities faced by specific age groups. This section provides an interactive visualization that explores median income across age categories, offering insights into how income evolves from early adulthood to retirement. By comparing age groups and tracking changes over time, we can better understand the economic realities of different generations and the broader implications for financial security and inequality.

There is a clear relationship between median income and age, reflecting the life cycle of an individual's career. Income increases through early adulthood and peaks during the 35-54 age range before starting to decline. This happens to correspond with what is traditionally considered the prime working years-the period when workers gain experience, often complete higher education or specialized skill training, and advance in their careers. Skilled professional capital will have built up over time, and the possibility of higher earnings is realized more in these middle parts of life.

On the other hand, the 15-24 age bracket has the smallest median income. This difference is explained by the high number of part-time and entry-level jobs in this age category because many are still in school or just starting their work careers. Paralleling that, the decrease in median income across the age 65-and-over group represents their movement into the retired stage of life when they would be dependent mostly on fixed incomes, like pensions or Social Security, rather than active wages.

The income plateau and subsequent decline beyond age 55 give rise to important considerations of economic security in older age. This finding underlines the importance of ample retirement savings and social support to ensure economic security in later life. It also may be informative to compare the 2022 and 2023 age-income distributions for evidence of how economic conditions-including inflation and/or labor market changes-affect different age groups over time.

This figure displays the interactions between age, career trajectory, and financial planning as a frame for considering life-span implications of economic inequality.

Darker shades represent states that have higher unemployment rates, while lighter shades show states that have relatively lower rates. In contrast, the highest unemployment rates are found in states of the South and parts of the West Coast, which may reflect structural challenges such as industry-specific job losses, higher cost of living pressures, or restricted access to employment opportunities in rural or economically depressed areas.

This also brings out the contribution of regional policies and industries to employment. For example, states that depend on tourism or oil production face fluctuations in demand and exogenous shocks. Further, variations among neighboring states also indicate that local governance, educational systems, and infrastructure investments influence the characteristics of the labor market.

Further investigation into either trends over time or more detailed demographic and industry-specific unemployment data could help explain these patterns. As such, this map calls for action in the form of targeted economic interventions and workforce development programs to address regional disparities and create more equitable opportunities across the nation.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP):

  • Definition: GDP measures the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country’s borders in a specific time period, usually annually or quarterly.
  • Scope: National level (e.g., the entire United States).
  • Purpose:
    • Provides a snapshot of the overall economic health of a country.
    • Used for comparing the economic performance of different countries.
  • Components:
    • Consumption: Spending by households.
    • Investment: Spending on capital goods like machinery, buildings, etc.
    • Government Expenditures: Government spending on goods and services.
    • Net Exports: Exports minus imports.
  • Example: The GDP of the United States in 2024 is approximately $28.78 trillion.

Gross State Product (GSP):

  • Definition: GSP measures the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a specific state in a given period. It is the state-level equivalent of GDP, measuring economic performance at a sub-national level.
  • Scope: State level (e.g., California, Texas).
  • Purpose:
    • Reflects the economic contribution of individual states to the national economy.
    • Useful for comparing the economic performance of different states within the same country.
  • Components: Same as GDP, but limited to a single state’s economy.
  • Example: Texas’s GSP in 2023 is approximately $2.6 trillion.

Key Differences Between GDP and GSP:

Feature GDP GSP
Geographic Focus National (entire country) State (individual states)
Level of Aggregation Macro-level (global comparisons) Micro-level (regional comparisons)
Use Case Compare countries or track overall national economic trends. Assess state-level contributions and disparities.

In essence, GSP is to a state what GDP is to a country. If you are analyzing economic data for individual states within a country (like the United States), the term GSP is appropriate. However, when referring to the aggregated economic performance of the entire country, you use GDP.
